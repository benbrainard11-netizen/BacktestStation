Level-Reaction Scalping in CME Equity Index
Futures
Executive summary
The strongest public evidence does not support the idea that a naked chart level, by itself, is a durable
post-2015 retail edge in ES, NQ, YM, or RTY. What is well supported is a narrower claim: salient levels matter
because they concentrate resting liquidity, stop-losses, benchmark execution, and hedging flows, and
those flows change the microstructure around the level. In other words, the tradable object is usually not
the line; it is the interaction between a salient line and queue state, imbalance, hidden liquidity, time of
day, and options regime. That distinction is the difference between something that survives realistic costs
and something that dies in a backtest. 
For a non-colocated retail trader using Rithmic with roughly 5–50 ms latency, the line between “capturable”
and “HFT-only” sits exactly where you would expect: reactive strategies that depend on being first to
respond to order-book events, hard-information shocks, or microsecond book changes are largely HFT
territory, while pre-positioned passive strategies that rest at salient prices before the touch remain
plausible if they target horizons of tens of seconds to a few minutes and clear a realistic fill/adverse-
selection hurdle. Speed-sensitive papers show profitable high-frequency technical signals degrade
materially with 50–200 ms delays; HFTs dominate hard-information processing; and market-makers use
speed upgrades mainly to reduce adverse selection. 
Your best research program is therefore not “Which levels bounce?” but “At which pre-computed salient
levels do I get a favorable combination of fill probability, post-fill drift, and retest behavior after
conditioning on regime?” The most defensible implementation is an MBO-reconstructed FIFO queue
model with competing-risk survival estimates for fill, miss, move-away, and adverse selection, calibrated
separately by level family, time of day, and options regime. The literature supports this modeling direction
strongly. 
Ranked level families for reaction trades
Below is the ranking I would use for reaction trades specifically, not for breakout or magnet trades. Each
item is labeled as documented with data, practitioner folklore with plausible mechanism, or folklore
likely false.
Large resting liquidity at the book, especially when reinforced by absorption or iceberg
behavior — documented with data. This is the cleanest microstructure evidence base. Queue
imbalance predicts near-term price moves; support/resistance-type prices coincide with depth
peaks; and iceberg liquidity changes order-flow and price-impact dynamics. This is fundamentally a 
reaction setup, but its useful horizon is usually milliseconds to a few seconds, which makes 
reactive entry fragile for retail. The version that can survive is the one where you post in advance

Round numbers and clustered price levels — documented with data. Price clustering is one of

Options strikes and dealer-gamma-conditioned strike behavior — documented with data, but
mostly for attraction/pinning and regime effects rather than “wall bounce” folklore. Classic
pinning at strikes on expiration is real in equities; newer 0DTE/SPX work shows that dealer gamma
state changes whether intraday price action is more reversing or more momentum-like. Positive
gamma supports reaction trades; negative gamma makes “walls” much less reliable and favors 
continuation after breach. The caveat is important: public evidence is much stronger for gamma-
state effects and expiration pinning than for social-media-style “gamma wall” charts. Plain “max
pain” as an intraday level tool is not a serious primary signal.
Prior-day high/low, overnight high/low, session extremes, and the opening range — 
practitioner folklore with plausible mechanism, with indirect academic support but little modern
peer-reviewed ES/NQ/YM/RTY evidence isolating the first-touch bounce itself. The mechanism is
strong: these are public focal points where traders cluster orders and stops. But the literature I
found is stronger on support/resistance depth, stop clustering, and opening-range breakout/
expansion than on the exact short-horizon bounce expectancy off prior-session extremes. For
reaction trades, these levels should be treated as candidate locations that need queue/flow
confirmation rather than as standalone edges. Naive first-touch fades likely fail after costs.
VWAP and VWAP bands — practitioner folklore with plausible mechanism. VWAP’s importance
as an institutional benchmark is beyond dispute, and large parent orders are a major reason VWAP
activity exists. What is not well documented in the primary literature is a stable, modern, short-
horizon reaction edge from fading or bouncing precisely at intraday VWAP in index futures. The
more credible interpretation is that VWAP is a benchmark / fair-value anchor / participation
magnet, not a magical support-resistance line. Standalone VWAP bounce strategies are likely
overfit after costs.
Prior swing highs/lows and “liquidity pools” — practitioner folklore with plausible mechanism.
The mechanism is credible because stop-losses do cluster at salient prior extrema and because
support/resistance-type levels can coincide with depth peaks. But I did not find a strong public,
modern, CME-equity-futures paper giving a portable effect size for “prior swing high/low sweep then
reverse.” This means the concept is reasonable, but the edge must be proven in your own data.

What the literature actually says about each level family
Prior-day high/low/close and overnight/session extremes. The case for these levels is mostly indirect in
the literature. Kavajecz and Odders-White show that support and resistance levels coincide with peaks in
depth on the book, which makes the mechanism for bounce-or-break behavior credible. Osler’s work shows
that salient support/resistance areas are tied to clustered stop-loss and take-profit orders, which explains
why the same level can behave like support on approach and like fuel once breached. What I did not find is
a strong modern paper on ES/NQ/YM/RTY that says, “first touch of prior-day high after x overnight path
yields y ticks over z seconds.” So the defensible label here is practitioner folklore with plausible
mechanism, not “documented turnkey edge.” 
Opening range. Academic evidence is meaningfully stronger for opening-range breakout than for
opening-range reaction fades. Holmberg, Lönnbark, and Lundström report significantly positive returns
for a mechanical ORB style, but Donninger’s S&P 500 futures paper is the right cold shower: several simple
published intraday strategies do not work as advertised under realistic assumptions. For your purpose that
means opening-range boundaries are more defensible as state variables — “are we in expansion or
failure?” — than as automatically fadeable levels. Post-2015, I would assume any pure OR-fade rule is fragile
unless it is filtered by time of day, queue state, and options regime. 
Round numbers. This is one of the best-supported families on your list. Harris documented pervasive price
clustering. Chiao and coauthors explicitly connect round-price limit-order clustering to excess depth and
barrier behavior . A later paper by Xiao Zhang reports economically meaningful predictability from daily
closes just above versus below round thresholds — about 24.6 bps next day and 46.1 bps over the
following week — and attributes it to limit-order clustering. Osler’s work adds the crucial short-horizon
nuance: salient round-number support/resistance can produce reversal before the break and 
acceleration after the break, because stops and take-profits are not arranged symmetrically around the
level. For index-futures scalping, that means round-number levels are best thought of as bifurcation
points: early touches can bounce, but once the break starts and stops fire, the trade construction should
flip from mean reversion to continuation. 
VWAP and VWAP bands. The academic literature strongly supports VWAP as an institutional execution
benchmark. Madhavan describes VWAP’s appeal as a forgiving benchmark; Kakade and coauthors note
that very large institutional trades are one of the main motivations behind VWAP activity; Duffie, Dworczak,
and Zhu use VWAP as a canonical benchmark-fixing mechanism. None of that automatically implies a
modern intraday bounce edge at VWAP in ES or NQ. The clean inference is narrower: because benchmarked
execution and participation algorithms benchmark against VWAP , price can be drawn toward it or treat it
as a reference around which participation changes. That makes VWAP more credible as an attraction/
context variable than as a primary reaction level. 
HVN/LVN/POC and volume profile. Public evidence here is thin. The best charitable interpretation is that
profile concepts are informal ways of describing previous acceptance and rejection, and therefore
sometimes proxy for where liquidity providers may be willing to re-engage. But compared with the

Options strikes, pinning, gamma walls, and max pain. Here the literature is real but often misused. Ni,
Pearson, and Poteshman show that on expiration dates, closing prices of optionable stocks cluster at strike
prices and that returns are altered by an average of at least 16.5 basis points. A later paper finds that
monthly pinning remained pervasive after weekly options were introduced, but that pinning was not
significant on weekly option expiration days, which is already an important reminder that these effects
change as market structure changes. More recent SPX/0DTE work sharpens the intuition relevant for futures
traders: dealer gamma is usually positive, positive gamma is associated with lower future intraday
volatility and stronger reversal, while negative gamma strengthens momentum; a Cboe study estimates
the maximum impact of options-market-maker gamma at about 3.3 annualized volatility points on a
daily basis and 6.4 points over 30-minute periods. That supports using your SPX/NDX/RUT/DJX chain data
to build gamma-state filters and strike salience scores. It does not validate social-media “gamma wall”
maps as precision trading levels. And “max pain” is a bad place to anchor a serious scalping strategy: even
where papers find some relation around expiration, the evidence is not the same thing as a robust intraday
futures level effect. 
Visible resting liquidity and hidden liquidity. This is the strongest microstructure substrate for a reaction
trader . Cont, Kukanov, and Stoikov show that short-horizon price changes are mainly driven by order-flow
imbalance at the best quotes, with a roughly linear relation whose slope is inversely proportional to depth.
Gould and Bonart find that queue imbalance improves one-tick-ahead prediction materially for large-tick
stocks, with classification performance improving by about 20–30% there. Kavajecz and Odders-White tie
technical levels to displayed depth peaks. On hidden liquidity, Zotikov and Antonov show that native and
synthetic CME icebergs can be detected from message data, and Frey and Sandås show that detected
iceberg orders materially change order flow and price impact. Two warnings matter for practice. First, 
reacting after you see the absorption is often too late without HFT-grade speed. Second, visible depth is
not the same thing as reliable liquidity; CME notes that in a volatile 2025 ES session, volume surged while
order-book depth fell sharply, which is a direct reminder that “big visible size” can evaporate or mislead. 
Prior swing highs/lows and stop pools. This is the modern “liquidity pool” meme, but its real content is
older and plainer: stop orders and take-profits cluster at salient prior prices, and once they trigger they can
generate price cascades. Osler’s papers are still the foundational evidence here. The problem is portability:
a stylized fact about stop clustering and trend propagation is not the same as a positive-expectancy ES
scalp after a sweep of a local high. So the right label is plausible mechanism, evidence gap on portable
effect size. 
Recommended queue and fill model from MBO
With CME-style MBO and FIFO matching, you should move beyond MBP-style probabilistic queue guesses
and reconstruct the exact displayed FIFO queue at each price, then add a small number of explicit
uncertainty terms for what MBO still does not give you: your own latency, hidden liquidity, and cancel
exposure after you decide to pull. CME’s own documentation says MBO shows individual order sizes
that make up total quantity at a price, including priority in the queue, and that books are built by
sorting by price and then order priority. CME equity-index futures are FIFO. Your own order assumption

execution ahead of you must be tracked exactly when the feed allows it. 
The model I would implement is a competing-risks survival model on top of exact queue
reconstruction. The structural part comes from queue-reactive theory: within short intervals where the
reference price is unchanged, model each price level as a state-dependent queue, with intensities for add,
cancel, and market-order consumption conditional on the current state of the book. Huang, Lehalle, and
Rosenbaum formalize this queue-reactive view; Cont, Kukanov, and Stoikov provide the core order-flow
framework; Moallemi and Yuan show why queue position itself has economic value and why adverse
selection rises with worse position; and newer fill-probability papers show that survival-style models and
deep survival models outperform simpler benchmarks. 
Concretely, define the state at order placement as follows: effective arrival time after a sampled send
latency; queue-ahead size at the chosen price; total size at the level; top-of-book or multi-level imbalance;
recent market-order intensity; recent cancel intensity; replenishment intensity; spread; local realized
volatility; time of day; level family label; first-touch versus retest; and options regime variables such as
nearby-strike open interest, gamma sign, and distance to large strikes. Because you have MBO, you can
also add event-level features that MBP cannot see: order-age distribution at the level, whether preceding
cancels were front-loaded, whether visible size replenishes immediately after trades, and whether native or
synthetic iceberg behavior is present. The MBO literature explicitly finds that MBO data is additive to LOB
features and useful for short-horizon forecasting. 
For the target, do not estimate just one fill probability. Estimate a full set of competing events over several
horizons: fill within 100 ms, 500 ms, 1 s, 5 s, 30 s, 2 min; move-away before fill; price-through without full fill;
and cancel-before-fill after your cancellation decision plus cancel latency. The survival papers are useful
precisely because they estimate the distribution of fill times, not just a binary label. Your “placed T
seconds before touch” study should therefore be event-aligned: for each level touch candidate, insert
synthetic orders T seconds before the first touch, apply a sampled latency, reconstruct the exact queue
ahead at arrival, and then roll forward event by event. 
For a retail order in ES, the most defensible queue assumption is the conservative one: joins last, and is
advanced only by reductions ahead of it. If you had only MBP , Rigtorp-style probabilistic queue estimation
or hftbacktest probability queue models would be defensible approximations. Since you have MBO, use
those only as fallbacks or as stress tests. Your remaining uncertainty is mainly hidden liquidity and your
actual order-to-exchange and exchange-to-cancel delays. Hidden liquidity should be handled with an
explicit iceberg/hide multiplier on effective queue-ahead and effective queue-behind, estimated from
observed replenishment and from detectable iceberg events. 
For adverse selection, estimate two quantities separately: E[mid move over h | filled] and E[mid move
over h | touched]. Do not substitute one for the other . The literature is clear that fills are not random.
Moallemi and Yuan treat adverse selection as a core component of queue value; Albers et al. report a
negative correlation between maker fill likelihood and post-fill returns; and “The Negative Drift of a Limit
Order Fill” makes the point directly that fills tend to coincide with adverse price movement. In practice that
means touched-but-not-filled is your baseline for “level reaction,” while filled is the economically relevant
outcome. The gap between the two is the tax your fill model must overcome.

My recommendation is therefore:
Use exact FIFO replay from MBO for displayed queue.
Overlay a competing-risks survival model for fill / miss / move-away.
Include explicit parameters for latency, hidden-liquidity multiplier, cancel latency, time of day, 
options regime, and level family.

Retail-latency economics and what is actually capturable
At your stated commission schedule, the commission hurdle alone is already meaningful. CME’s contract
specs imply one ES tick is $12.50, one NQ tick is $5, one RTY tick is $5, and one YM tick is $5. So a $3.80
round-turn is about 0.30 ES ticks but about 0.76 ticks in NQ, RTY, and YM before you even count spread
crossing, slippage, or adverse selection. That is one reason the same strategy can look fine in ES and
mediocre in NQ/RTY/YM even before any alpha difference appears. 
The speed literature makes the main dividing line very clear . Scholtus and van Dijk find that for profitable
high-frequency technical rules, a 200 ms delay is enough to reduce performance significantly, and on low-
volatility days a 50 ms delay already matters. Budish, Cramton, and Shim show why: at sub-second
horizons, continuous-time markets create mechanical arbitrage rents that mainly reward speed. Sarah
Zhang’s “Need for Speed” shows HFTs dominate non-HFTs in processing hard information. So if the trade
requires you to see a book event and then react before others, 5–50 ms is usually not “slow but okay”; it is
often the wrong game. 
The setups that remain plausible for you are the latency-robust ones:
Latency-robust constructions. Resting passive orders placed well ahead of touch at pre-computed levels;
failed-break or sweep-reversal trades where the entry is already working in queue before the flip is obvious;
rotation trades in positive-gamma environments where the expected post-fill path is more reverting; and
trades with holding periods long enough that 5–50 ms is a second-order issue relative to minutes of
subsequent movement. These rely on salience, queue economics, and regime rather than on being first. 
Latency-fragile constructions. Reactive book-imbalance trades, reactive absorption trading after the
absorption is visible, trade-sign burst responses, cash-futures lead-lag responses, news or macro-release
reactions, and most sub-second “I watched DOM and jumped in” tactics. Queue-imbalance and order-flow
signals can be powerful, but papers on order-book alpha consistently emphasize very short effective
horizons. Kolm, Turiel, and Westray find the effective horizon of stock-specific order-book forecasts is about 
two average price changes, which is another way of saying the horizon is short and decays fast. 
On the narrow question “can seconds-to-minutes mean-reversion scalps at reference levels clear costs
without colocation?”, the honest answer from the public record is: sometimes in principle, but probably
not as a naked ruleset in today’s most liquid index futures. The evidence for simple intraday tactics in
S&P futures under realistic assumptions is poor; maker fills are negatively selected; and the cost hurdle is

Stops, sweeps, and retests
The best hard evidence here still comes from Carol Osler . She documents that stop-loss orders cluster at
salient levels and that they can produce rapid, self-reinforcing price cascades; the response to stop-loss
orders lasts hours, longer than the response to take-profit orders. She also documents clustering of stop-
loss and take-profit orders around support/resistance-type prices and round numbers. For a level trader ,
that has two direct implications. First, stops placed just beyond obvious public levels are where positive-
feedback flow is most likely to hit. Second, a breach of a salient level is not symmetric: once stops trigger ,
the trade construction should often switch from reversion logic to continuation logic. 
That said, I did not find a strong public quantitative paper giving a portable answer to “optimal stop offset
beyond the level in ES” or “base rate of retest within k ticks after first rejection.” This is a genuine evidence
gap. The mechanism is supported; the exact calibration is not. For your own research, this is one of the
highest-value things to estimate from the data you already have: conditional retest rates after a first sweep,
as a function of time of day, level family, initial overshoot, and options regime. Because you have MBO, you
can go further and condition on whether the sweep was accompanied by replenishment / iceberg-like
absorption or by one-sided queue depletion with no refill. That distinction is likely more valuable than the
raw fact of a sweep. 
My prior for stop placement is therefore simple and fairly ruthless: for reaction trades at salient levels, a
stop that sits exactly where public folklore says it should sit is often donating edge to the market. A better
design is to choose offsets from your own data by minimizing expected loss = stop-hit probability × stop
size + miss cost + adverse-fill cost, estimated separately by level type. That is an inference from the
literature, but it is the correct one. 
Backtest failure modes that will fool you
The canonical lie is fill-on-touch optimism. Replay systems and bar data often make it look as if touching
your price means you traded. That is false in FIFO markets unless you are actually at or near the front of the
queue. HftBacktest’s documentation is explicit that queue position matters, that replay backtesting
assumes no market impact, and that some fill assumptions — especially around liquidity-taking and partial
fills — can be unrealistic. Rigtorp’s queue-position note explains why MBP feeds force you into a model; with
MBO you do not need that excuse. 
The next lie is ignoring adverse selection. It is not enough to know whether you would have been filled;
you must know what happens because you were filled. The recent literature is blunt: fill likelihood and post-
fill returns trade off negatively, and limit-order fills exhibit negative drift. If your backtest counts spread
capture and ignores post-fill drift, it will overstate PnL exactly where the strategy feels smartest. 
Then comes latency blindness. Your model must separate signal time, decision time, order-arrival time,
and cancel-effective time. Scholtus shows even tens of milliseconds can change outcomes in short-horizon
technical trading. If your backtest lets you cancel on the event that told you to cancel, instead of after
realistic round-trip delay, you are giving yourself a free option the real market will not honor .

Another big lie is bar-based last look. Message-level order-book strategies must be tested message by
message. Event-based LOB simulation papers stress that identifying which LOB events caused the move is
essential, and Brownian or bar-based approximations do not capture this. If your strategy condition is “price
touched level and then closed back inside the bar ,” but your order had to exist before that bar closed, the
backtest has already leaked information. 
The final lie I would highlight is conditioning on touched levels after the fact. This one is not always
named in the literature, but it is deadly in level research. If you construct a set of levels using future
interaction or “valid levels that were respected,” you have imported survivorship and look-ahead into the
sample definition itself. In your setting, every candidate level must be generated ex ante, timestamped,
and allowed to fail silently. Anything else is storytelling. This is partly an inference, but it follows directly
from the event-driven and queue-value literature. 
What practitioners say works now, what I believe, and what I would
not bother with
The practitioner consensus that deserves respect is narrower than the marketing language suggests.
Recent broker/platform material converges on a sensible idea: prior-day highs/lows, overnight highs/lows,
and value areas are a roadmap, not “magic prediction lines.” Open-source and practitioner execution tools
also keep emphasizing order-book imbalance and queue position as the operational variables that
determine whether a limit order actually pays. That part is consistent with the literature. 
Where practitioner lore is probably right is on three filters. First, first touch usually beats later touch, not
because “the market remembers” but because displayed liquidity gets consumed and informed traders
learn. Second, time of day matters: open, macro-release windows, and the close are not the same game as
midday rotation. Third, visible absorption is not enough; you need to distinguish genuine replenishment/
iceberg behavior from fleeting size or spoof-like depth. These are plausible mechanisms, and the iceberg-
detection literature on CME supports the third point in particular . But public evidence that quantifies these
exact practitioner slogans in ES/NQ/YM/RTY remains thin. 
Where practitioner lore is often wrong is in treating visible size as truth. CME’s own discussion of 2025 ES
conditions shows that volume can surge while order-book depth collapses, which is a clean reminder that
visible liquidity is not stable liquidity. So “there’s a big bid there” is a necessary observation, not a sufficient
one. You need its persistence, refill behavior, and post-trade replenishment pattern. 
My do-not-bother list is short but firm:
Do not bother with standalone volume-profile bounces. Public academic support is too thin. If HVN/LVN/
POC adds value, let it earn its place only as a conditioning feature on top of stronger primitives. 
Do not bother with “max pain” as a primary intraday futures level. Use actual strike open interest,
same-day maturity structure, and dealer-gamma estimates instead. Classical pinning and recent gamma-
state work are real; “max pain” retail lore is not the serious version of that idea.

Do not bother with naked VWAP fades. Keep VWAP as context, not as your main signal. Benchmark

Open questions and limitations
Several things you asked for are genuinely under-documented in the public literature. I did not find a
strong modern peer-reviewed estimate of the short-horizon bounce expectancy of prior-day highs/lows, 
overnight highs/lows, or prior swing highs/lows specifically in CME equity-index futures. I also did not
find a strong public paper quantifying the optimal stop offset beyond a salient level or the base rate of
retest within k ticks after first rejection in ES/NQ/YM/RTY. Those are exactly the places where your own
data can create real edge because the public literature does not give away a ready-made answer . 
The strongest evidence base in your problem sits around queue economics, adverse selection, speed
sensitivity, order clustering, and options-hedging regimes. If you build the study so those pieces
dominate and the named “level” is only one feature among them, you will be doing bulletproof work. If you
build it around mystical lines with optimistic fills, you will be building a very elegant lie.
