Cross-Asset Order-Flow Divergence Forecasting for
Futures
Core judgment
The evidence-backed part of your thesis is not “cross-asset divergence” in the loose retail sense. It is a much
narrower proposition: short-lived, lagged, cross-asset predictability exists after you convert each
asset’s book into a depth-aware pressure process, remove the common flow factor inside the
complex, align instruments in event time, and only trade when the price relationship itself is still
structurally intact. The academic support is strongest for event-based OFI, multi-level OFI, queue
imbalance, microprice, and short-horizon lagged cross-asset OFI; it is much weaker for generic
contemporaneous cross-impact stories, and essentially absent in the serious literature for the chart-pattern
layer as a standalone alpha source. 
Two skeptical conclusions matter . First, raw contemporaneous cross-impact is probably not your edge.
Once order flow is integrated across levels and common order-flow components are modeled, a large
fraction of apparent cross-impact disappears; in one multi-asset impact study, additional cross-impact
terms explain less than 1% of impact beyond idiosyncratic OFI plus a common market flow factor . What
remains useful is lagged cross-asset OFI, and even that decays rapidly. Second, static book snapshots are
too slow a representation. The useful horizon is much closer to event time than to a fixed 15–60 minute
bar horizon; Kolm, Turiel, and Westray explicitly report an effective horizon of roughly two average price
changes for stock-specific forecasts from LOB data. 
For your current data mix, the highest-probability research path is a hybrid stack: engineered
microstructure features as the hard baseline, gradient boosting as the benchmark model on aggregated
pair and complex features, and a compact LOB-aware sequence encoder only where raw MBO exists and
genuinely adds incremental OOS value. The literature is supportive of deep models on raw LOB states, but
benchmark studies also show large performance drops on new data and across datasets, including worse
transfer from stock-style benchmarks to futures-style data. That is exactly the setting where large “global”
architectures get overhyped. 
Divergence signals that are worth your research budget
Highest-priority signals
Integrated multi-level OFI divergence should be near the top of the list. Best-level OFI is already strong,
but multiple studies show that order flow deeper in the book adds explanatory and forecasting power when
handled properly. Xu, Gould, and Howison find that out-of-sample fit improves as additional price levels are
added to MLOFI, while Cont, Cucuringu, and Zhang show that an integrated OFI built from several top levels
explains impact better than best-level OFI alone. For your use case, the signal is not just “asset A OFI minus
asset B OFI,” but depth-normalized, lag-aligned integrated OFI residuals within a complex.

Idiosyncratic-versus-common flow divergence is the single missing construct that most often gets
overlooked by discretionary order-flow researchers. Capponi and Cont show that much of what looks like
cross-impact can instead be explained by common order-flow components across assets, with idiosyncratic
OFI plus a marketwide OFI factor doing most of the work. In plain terms: if ES and NQ are both being
carried by the same common flow factor , “divergence” is mostly fake unless one contract’s idiosyncratic
pressure materially disagrees with the complex factor . That makes residualization against a complex-level
flow factor a first-class feature, not a cleanup step. 
Queue depletion hazard and microprice divergence are strongly supported at very short horizons.
Queue imbalance at the best bid and ask has significant predictive power for the direction of the next mid-
price move, especially in large-tick settings, and Stoikov’s microprice framework treats fair short-term value
as an expectation of future mid-price conditional on book state rather than simply a weighted midpoint.
Cartea, Donnelly, and Jaimungal further show that volume imbalance predicts the sign of the next market
order and immediate post-order price changes. That gives you a very actionable cross-asset construction:
compare microprice-to-mid dislocation or next-move hazard across members of a complex, rather than
comparing raw price alone. 
Lagged cross-asset OFI spillovers are real, but fragile. Cont, Cucuringu, and Zhang find that lagged cross-
asset OFIs improve forecasts of future returns, but that the effect is concentrated at short horizons and
decays quickly. Garrison, Jain, and Paddrik find a positive but short-lived relationship in cross-asset order-
flow activity between SPY and E-mini futures, with cross-asset market order flow becoming especially
relevant during volatile periods. Rosenberg and Traub show that futures order flow can contain incremental
information for spot returns and even lead spot prices in some FX settings. The implication is blunt: if your
divergence only works on a minute bar without precise lag alignment, assume it is contaminated. 
Secondary but evidence-backed signals
Resiliency and replenishment asymmetry deserve more attention than they get. Large defines resiliency
as the speed and probability with which the book refills after shocks, and Lo and Hall quantify liquidity
replenishment through impulse responses in high-frequency VARs. If one asset absorbs repeated
aggressive flow and refills almost immediately while its peer thins or fails to refill, that divergence is
microstructurally meaningful. This is especially relevant to your “one asset holds while the correlated one
runs” thesis: the holding asset is often not merely “stuck”; it is expressing higher same-side replenishment
intensity. 
Book-shape asymmetry is useful, but less universally robust than OFI. Several papers document predictive
content in the slope or supply schedule of the order book for future trade prices, volatility, and speed of
trading. This matters cross-asset because two contracts can show similar top-level imbalance while having
very different depth elasticity behind the touch. The tradeable form is not raw slope; it is the difference in
price-pressure-per-unit-flow implied by each asset’s current book shape. I would rank this below MLOFI
and microprice, but above most discretionary structure heuristics. 
Hidden-liquidity and refill asymmetry are real, especially in futures, but the evidence is narrower and
more product-specific. Avellaneda, Reed, and Stoikov explicitly model hidden liquidity at level I and show
that best-quote information improves prediction of the next price move when hidden liquidity is accounted
for . On CME, iceberg detection and prediction research shows that native and synthetic iceberg activity can

Useful, but easier to overhype
Order-flow surprise relative to a self-exciting baseline is promising but still more niche. Taranto et al.
show that price impact is better modeled when one separates the predictable component of order flow
from the unexpected one, and newer Hawkes-based work forecasts the near-term distribution of OFI by
modeling lagged dependence in bid and ask event flows. In your framework, the right object is not raw OFI
but unexpected OFI, especially cross-asset unexpected OFI. I would treat this as a second-wave refinement
after the simpler residualized MLOFI and microprice stack is fully explored. 
A futures-specific exploratory signal is divergence between outright flow and implied/spread-derived
liquidity contribution. CME’s matching documentation shows that implied and outright sources interact in
the allocation and priority process, and that many products are not simple FIFO books. That does not yet
amount to broad empirical proof of alpha, but it does mean that for rates, grains, and spread-heavy
products, outright visible-book pressure can be misleading unless you model the implied side of the
market. This is structurally plausible, but I would label it exploratory rather than evidence-backed. 
Building the features so they survive out of sample
The canonical starting point remains the Cont–Kukanov–Stoikov event-based OFI, because it explicitly
includes limit orders, market orders, and cancellations, rather than collapsing everything to signed
traded volume. In their stylized block-book setup, price changes depend on the net imbalance of these
three order types, and in the empirical regressions price changes are well explained by OFI with a
coefficient that scales inversely with depth. That last point matters for cross-asset work: raw OFI is not
comparable across instruments unless you normalize by local impact or depth. 
For multi-level construction, Xu, Gould, and Howison provide a clean template. At each event time , define
side-specific level- queue changes piecewise according to whether the level price improves, stays the
same, or worsens. Their MLOFI entry is then the sum over events of ,
and  collapses to standard OFI. The practical translation is straightforward: build a level vector, not
a scalar; then compress it with ridge, PCA, or a learned linear layer into an integrated OFI that is more
stable than naively throwing all levels into OLS. 
Queue imbalance should be treated as a hazard variable, not a durable directional signal. Gould and
Bonart define queue imbalance at time  as the normalized difference between best bid and ask queue
sizes, , and show significant predictive power for the next mid-price move. Cartea,
Donnelly, and Jaimungal show related volume-imbalance measures predict the sign of the next market
order and immediate price changes. But this edge is strongest at one-tick or next-event horizons, stronger
in large-tick assets, and should be expected to decay quickly once you aggregate too coarsely. 
For microprice, keep two versions. The first is the standard “naive” microprice, equivalently a queue-
weighted quote or  under fixed spread. The second is Stoikov’s more principled
microprice as the limit of expected future midprices under a Markov transition model on imbalance states.

The decay problem is real, and the way around it is mostly representational, not magical. You should prefer 
event time to fixed wall-clock windows whenever possible; use multiple short exponential kernels rather
than a single trailing sum; normalize OFI by an impact proxy such as inverse depth or a rolling local ;
and define some horizons in number of price changes rather than only seconds or minutes. That advice is
directly consistent with the literature: Cont et al. link impact to inverse depth, Cont et al. find lagged cross-
asset OFI decays rapidly, and Kolm et al. place the useful forecast horizon at roughly two average price
changes. 
The robust/fragile split is fairly clear . Robust: event-based OFI, integrated multi-level OFI, queue imbalance
as a next-event hazard, microprice gap, replenishment intensity, and common-factor residualization. 
Fragile: CVD or signed volume without book context, static book snapshots on minute bars, raw
contemporaneous cross-impact coefficients, and universal feature sets that ignore matching algorithm
differences. The literature keeps returning to the same lesson: prices react to the whole book event
process, not just prints.

Model classes and the real value of time-series foundation models
What should be the hard baseline
For engineered cross-asset microstructure features, gradient boosting should be your hard benchmark.
This is not because trees are elegant. It is because on medium-sized tabular problems, tree ensembles
remain extremely competitive and often outperform generic deep tabular approaches, especially when
features are already high-level and irregular . With one year of MBP-1 across 28 symbols and only about 120
days of MBO on four index contracts, you are still much closer to “medium, structured, heterogeneous
tabular” than to the data regime where giant end-to-end attention models obviously dominate. 
What should be the deep baseline
For raw LOB or event-stream inputs, the evidence still favors LOB-aware compact architectures over
generic sequence maximalism. DeepLOB established that convolutional filters plus temporal modeling can
extract robust predictive structure from raw LOB states and generalize across instruments better than many
earlier approaches. Transformer-based LOB models can outperform earlier architectures on some
benchmarks, but broader benchmark work also shows that many models degrade substantially on new
data and that rankings can change between stock-style and futures-style datasets. In other words: a
transformer can win, but not because it is a transformer; it wins when its inductive bias matches the book. 
A sensible architecture for your problem is hierarchical and multi-resolution: an asset-level event encoder
at the native clock, pooling to short regular intervals such as 250 ms, 1 s, and 5 s, followed by sparse cross-
asset fusion inside each complex. That fusion can be attention-based, graph-based, or even a learned linear
mixer; the most important design choice is sparsity by complex and lag-awareness, not global attention
across everything. That is an inference from the literature rather than an established theorem, but it is
strongly aligned with what the evidence does show: cross-asset effects are local, short-lived, and dominated
by common factors plus a few idiosyncratic residual relationships.

Honest assessment of Chronos, TimesFM, Moirai, and TTM
The official TSFM papers are impressive on broad regular-grid forecasting benchmarks. Chronos learns via
value tokenization and language-model style training; TimesFM is a decoder-only forecasting model trained
on a very large corpus; Moirai tackles cross-frequency and any-variate forecasting; TTM is a tiny pretrained
multivariate model designed for efficient transfer . On their home turf—generic time-series forecasting—
they are serious models. 
But the finance-specific evidence is much less flattering for the generic, off-the-shelf story. A recent
comprehensive finance study finds that off-the-shelf TSFMs perform poorly in zero-shot and fine-tuning
settings on financial returns, while TSFMs pretrained from scratch on financial data achieve substantial
gains. A separate multivariate finance study finds that pretrained TTM can be sample-efficient and can beat
naive baselines on some tasks such as equity spread prediction and volatility forecasting, but specialized
models still match or exceed it in two of three tasks. That is the honest center of gravity right now: domain-
specific pretraining helps; generic zero-shot finance miracles do not. 
On financial microstructure or spread-resolution tasks, I would treat generic TSFMs as auxiliary
baselines, not core production models. Use them, if at all, on regularly sampled derived series such as
one-second or one-minute pressure residuals, spread residuals, or complex flow factors. Do not expect
them to ingest raw event-time MBO and suddenly discover edge. Their architectures and training corpora
were designed for broad forecasting corpora, not native message-by-message auction dynamics. Emerging
dedicated LOB foundation models such as LOBERT are interesting, but they are early and not yet evidence-
backed the way OFI, queue imbalance, and classical LOB models are. 
If you insist on a TSFM experiment, the least self-deceptive setup is: fine-tune TTM or Moirai on stationary
spread and pressure residual series, compare against GBDT and compact local sequence models, and
judge only after costs and actual holdout periods. Zero-shot should be treated as a calibration curiosity, not
an alpha thesis. 
Labeling the objective and gating the regime
For your target, the cleanest formulation is a competing-risks first-touch label. The classic triple-barrier
method is still the right conceptual starting point because it is path-aware and naturally encodes take-profit,
stop, and timeout. But for a cross-asset divergence trade, the upper barrier should not just be a symmetric
return multiple. It should be the first touch of an ex ante liquidity objective defined at time : a pre-
existing spread reversion band, a session-to-date profile level, a visible book objective, or a current
microprice-implied fair-value zone. If you already know the side from the divergence sign, the label should
mostly answer: did the trade resolve to the objective before adverse excursion, timeout, or regime
break?

There are two caveats. First, triple-barrier logic has been widely adopted, but newer critiques point out that
it can behave badly when implemented on fixed-resolution bars rather than on the true path. That matters
enormously for your use case. If the objective is a liquidity draw, tick or event-path labeling is
mandatory; bar-high/bar-low approximations are much too crude around microstructure horizons. Second,
meta-labeling is useful only if the primary side model already has merit. The practitioner and empirical
literature around meta-labeling is consistent on that point: it is a filter and sizing layer , not a way to rescue a
bad primary signal. 
Your no-lookahead requirement means the liquidity objective must be frozen using only information
available at decision time. Previous-session volume profile levels are safe; full-session levels are not.
Current-session HVN/LVN or POC can be used only if computed from flow accumulated up to now, not from
later prints. The same logic applies to any “liquidity draw” target: the target level can move only if the
trading rule would also have allowed it to move in real time. For cross-asset models, this usually means the
objective layer should be a deterministic function of the present and past state, with all future path
uncertainty delegated to the label outcome itself. 
For regime gating, I would separate qualification from monitoring. Qualification is the offline question: is
there a long-run relation at all? Here, cointegration tests that allow breaks, such as Gregory–Hansen, are
more appropriate than naive stationary-spread screening because they explicitly allow for regime shift in
slope or intercept. Monitoring is the online question: has the relation broken now? Wagner and Wied
develop a residual-based monitoring procedure for structural change in cointegrating relationships, and
related work extends monitoring from stationarity-to-spurious transitions and to broader sequential
monitoring of cointegrating regressions. These methods are much closer to the problem you actually have
than a rolling correlation threshold. 
In real time, I would monitor at least four objects: the cointegration residual; the hedge ratio or loading
vector; the lead-lag estimate; and the variance of the idiosyncratic flow residuals. For the online detector
itself, BOCPD, CUSUM, and MOSUM are the practical workhorses. BOCPD gives a posterior over run length
and is attractive when you want a probabilistic break score; CUSUM and MOSUM remain efficient for small
shifts in mean or parameter constancy and are easy to implement as hard gates. If any of those objects
destabilize, cut the signal or downweight it. That is how you avoid the “fade a dying relationship” failure
mode. 
The failure modes that usually kill these models out of sample
The first killer is fake lead-lag from clocks, liquidity, and synchronization choices. The Epps effect and
asynchronous sampling can make correlations collapse or generate artificial leadership. Huth and Abergel
explicitly prefer Hayashi–Yoshida because it bypasses asynchrony and artificial liquidity lead-lag, and
Hoffmann, Rosenbaum, and Yoshida formalize lead-lag estimation under non-synchronous sampling. If you
align everything on minute bars or previous-tick interpolation, you can manufacture a beautiful signal that
is nothing but timestamp bias. 
The second killer is mistaking common flow for divergence. This is the most important conceptual trap in
cross-asset order-flow work. If you do not separate common complex flow from idiosyncratic pressure, you
are not predicting relative resolution—you are usually just measuring the same complex-wide shove twice.
Capponi and Cont’s results are a direct warning here, and Cont, Cucuringu, and Zhang add that lagged
effects exist but are short-lived. If you fit a large model to raw pairwise interactions without residualization,

The third killer is product-specific queue semantics. CME MBO gives queue position and full depth, but
matching rules vary materially. Some products are FIFO-like; others use top-order plus pro-rata logic;
residual allocation can revert to FIFO; implied and outright sources interact in the same price level. A queue-
imbalance or cancel/add feature learned on one matching rule will not mean the same thing on another .
The signal layer therefore needs product metadata: matching algorithm, implied order participation, top-
order behavior , and whether your fill model even makes sense under the product’s allocation logic. 
The fourth killer is visible-book naivety in the presence of hidden or synthetic liquidity. Icebergs,
reserve replenishment, and implied spread-generated liquidity make visible size an incomplete state
description. CME-specific iceberg detection work shows native and synthetic iceberg activity can be inferred,
which is precisely why visible absorption by itself is not enough. In futures complexes with meaningful
implied activity, the book you see is only part of the liquidity process you are trading against. 
The fifth killer is intraday nonstationarity you treat as noise instead of structure. Commodity futures
work shows intraday price impact varies materially, with higher volume and lower impact around
settlement, and cross-asset order-flow relations strengthen during volatile periods. Macro-news work
shows simultaneous high-frequency response across stock, bond, and FX futures to real-time
announcements. If your model pretends that 10:02 a.m. and the minute before settlement are the same
microstructure state, it will average away precisely the conditions under which cross-asset divergence
matters most. 
The sixth killer is contract and venue migration around roll or thin-liquidity states. Price discovery in
futures depends on where informed flow actually trades; thinly traded futures can be poor discovery
venues, and the dominant venue can shift over time with transparency and liquidity conditions. Ready and
Ready explicitly work with near-to-maturity high-volume contracts, and Rosenberg and Traub show the
dominant information share can shift between futures and spot over time as transparency changes. Cross-
asset pressure comparisons are therefore only meaningful if the contracts being compared are the
contracts where discovery is actually occurring. 
The final killer is execution asymmetry masquerading as alpha. CME itself notes that exact queue
position is valuable for trades requiring resting orders in multiple instruments to manage execution risk. In
cross-asset futures trading, legging risk, queue aging, pro-rata allocation, and implied-source interactions
can turn a predictive signal into a negative-expectancy strategy if the execution model is naive. For this class
of strategy, the fill model is part of the alpha model. 
The research agenda that looks most defensible from the literature is therefore narrow and concrete: start
with event-time, multi-level, depth-normalized local pressure; strip out the complex common flow
factor; align assets with Hayashi–Yoshida-style lag estimation; predict objective resolution, not outright
direction; and gate aggressively on cointegration and parameter stability. That is the part of cross-asset
order-flow divergence that is evidence-backed. Everything else should be assumed guilty until it beats that
baseline after costs.
